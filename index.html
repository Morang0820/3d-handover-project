01 — Abstract

Abstract

Three-dimensional product design increasingly relies on asynchronous collaboration across distributed teams and time zones. When a designer begins a new shift, they must review hours of spatial work completed by a remote colleague — understanding what changed, when, and why — before contributing their own work. Existing tools fall short: 2D logs and passive video recordings fail to convey spatial relationships and design intent, while replacing these with full immersive 3D recordings reviewed inside an HMD introduces cybersickness and cognitive overload from rapid scrubbing.

We present a hybrid LED Wall + HMD system that addresses both limitations simultaneously. Designers navigate a 2D keyframe timeline using a mid-air dial gesture and receive visual and haptic cues at keyframes. When a keyframe warrants closer inspection, the designer pulls the corresponding 3D object into the HMD view for immersive examination, then pushes it back to return to 2D navigation. A 3×2 within-subjects pilot study (N = 6) asks: How do input modality and visual-haptic cue presence affect keyframe navigation performance, workflow reconstruction, and subjective workload during 3D work handover? Results reveal a compelling dichotomy between objective efficiency and subjective preference, with important implications for XR temporal navigation design.

02 — System

Interaction Design

The interaction follows a four-step flow that keeps immersive use short and purposeful, reducing cybersickness while preserving the spatial richness of 3D design history.

STEP 01

Browse timeline

Scroll through keyframes on the LED Wall using a mid-air dial gesture. The user first engages the gesture, then scrubs by rotating the wrist; clockwise and counter-clockwise map to forward and backward timeline movement.

STEP 02

Receive cues

At every keyframe arrival, a uniform visual flash and object highlight fire together with a short haptic pulse on the bHaptics glove, drawing attention without interrupting navigation.

STEP 03

Inspect in 3D

Pull a moment into 3D space via a Pull-to-Spawn action — engagement posture combined with hand movement toward the body — for closer spatial inspection in the HMD.

STEP 04

Return to 2D

Push-to-Dismiss sends the 3D object back by moving the hand away from the body; resume 2D timeline scrubbing.

Cognitive Design Rationale

The system separates ventral stream processing (object recognition via foveal keyframe content, 0–5°) from dorsal stream processing (gesture, haptic events), preventing a single channel from being overloaded during the dual task of simultaneous navigation and content recognition. Visual flash cues fire in the parafoveal zone (5–15°) without requiring a saccade; the full LED Wall timeline provides peripheral context (15°+).

Three cognitive load principles from LaViola et al. [8] are applied: a staged approach (2D overview first, 3D only on demand), a physical metaphor (wrist rotation maps intuitively to timeline scrubbing, like a jog wheel), and haptic offloading (keyframe arrival delivered through touch, freeing the visual channel for content recognition).

Multimodal Cue Design

For the experimental cue manipulation, one cue event was used: keyframe arrival. In the cue-on condition, every keyframe arrival produced a uniform visual flash and object highlight, paired with a short haptic pulse on the bHaptics glove. Additional visual states for overshoot and boundary feedback were implemented in the prototype but were not treated as separate cue conditions in the final 3×2 evaluation.

03 — Prototype

Prototype

The prototype implements the full interaction pipeline on a physical LED Wall with a Meta Quest 3 HMD (Unity 6000.3.4f1). Three input methods are compared: pinch gesture, controller, and the proposed mid-air dial (wrist rotation). All three control the same logical keyframe timeline, presented as a 2D overview on the LED Wall and viewed through the HMD passthrough environment. Haptic feedback is delivered via bHaptics TactGlove devices.

The dial gesture evolved from an initial curled-finger pose with slight flexion — which caused hand-tracking errors with haptic gloves — to a pinch-to-engage posture for reliable activation, with timeline position controlled by wrist rotation. The pinch posture served only as an engagement trigger, distinct from the separate pinch input condition evaluated in the user study.

04 — Evaluation

Evaluation

System Evaluation

System performance was evaluated across two critical latency dimensions prior to the user study.

Hand tracking latency (Meta Quest 3): Characterised against Godden et al. [2] — average positional error 1.11 cm; latency range 14.4–220.5 ms. Intermittent spikes toward 220 ms risk disrupting perceived continuity of the dial gesture during fine-grained scrubbing near target keyframes.

LED Wall display latency: ~70 ms (measured via debug logging). Cumulative end-to-end latency may compound to a perceptible level during rapid navigation, and is a non-trivial confound for elevated overshoot rates observed in the user study.

User Study Design

The experiment adopted a 3×2 within-subjects factorial design, crossing three input methods with two cue conditions (6 conditions total). Six adult participants aged 20–35 took part in the pilot study; each completed five trials per condition for 30 trials total. Statistical analysis used ART ANOVA (confirmed by Shapiro-Wilk normality tests) and the Friedman test for ordinal data (α = 0.05).

Modality-Dependent Feedback Effect (Question Score)

While main effects were negligible, a large but non-significant interaction trend between modality and feedback was found for Question Score (F(2, 30) = 2.656, p = .086, η²p = 0.150).

Adding feedback descriptively improved scores for Controller (1.83 → 2.33) and Pinch (1.67 → 2.00), but was associated with a descriptive decrease for Dial (2.33 → 1.50). This suggests feedback utility may be modality-dependent — should be interpreted as an exploratory signal rather than confirmed evidence.

06 — Discussion

Discussion

Performance vs. Preference

The most striking result is the disconnect between objective efficiency and subjective experience. The controller dominated objectively — large effect sizes for both TKAT (η²p = 0.449) and overshoot rate (η²p = 0.505) — yet this advantage did not translate into statistically significant superiority in any subjective metric including NASA-TLX, Intuitiveness, or Ease of Use. Contrary to initial hypotheses, the proposed dial-with-cue condition did not outperform the controller on objective navigation metrics.

Disturbance ratings suggest that handheld controllers may be efficient for 1D timeline manipulation but can introduce a subjective sense of physical intrusion in spatial computing environments. Hand-based modalities, though slower, showed descriptive advantages: pinch was rated easiest for target finding (mimicking natural pointing and grasping), while the dial earned the highest willingness-to-use ratings, suggesting the jog-wheel mental model is intuitive for temporal scrubbing — though control gain and range of motion require further optimization.

The Modality-Dependent Role of Sensory Feedback

Our pilot findings caution against assuming that sensory feedback universally improves XR interactions. For controller and pinch — where physical constraints or tactile cues are limited — feedback descriptively improved question scores, suggesting a useful confirmation signal for target acquisition. For the dial, adding feedback was descriptively associated with lower question scores. One possible interpretation is a timing mismatch between wrist-rotation control and the externally generated haptic cue. This result suggests, rather than conclusively demonstrates, that feedback should be designed in relation to the input modality rather than applied uniformly.

Design Implications

IMPLICATION 01

Match modality to task demand

For high-speed precision tasks, physical controllers remain the strongest option on objective metrics. For ambient or intermittent interactions where low intrusiveness is prioritized, bare-hand input may be preferable — provided interaction duration is short and gesture mapping is carefully calibrated.

IMPLICATION 02

Tailor feedback to modality affordances

Sensory feedback should supplement missing tactile information for modalities such as pinch, while avoiding overload or timing mismatch in modalities that already possess strong physical metaphors such as dial-based input.

IMPLICATION 03

Consider adaptive modality switching

Future systems should explore seamlessly transitioning between high-precision controller input and low-friction hand tracking based on the user's immediate context — rapid bulk navigation vs. careful inspection near target keyframes.

Limitations & Future Work

LIMITATION 01

Small pilot study (N = 6)

Limited statistical power means several medium-to-large effects did not reach significance. A larger study is needed to confirm observed trends. Medium-to-large effect sizes that did not reach significance should be interpreted as design signals rather than confirmatory evidence.

LIMITATION 02

Simplified task scenario

The study used a controlled timeline-navigation task rather than a realistic professional handover scenario with annotations, object dependencies, and decision rationales.

LIMITATION 03

Hardware latency confounds

Quest 3 hand tracking introduces intermittent latency spikes (up to 220.5 ms) and the LED Wall added ~70 ms. Future systems should incorporate latency compensation and more stable hand tracking to isolate gesture effects.

LIMITATION 04

Uniform cue design

Results suggest cue utility is modality-dependent, especially for dial. Future work should explore adaptive cue timing and modality-specific feedback rather than applying the same cue pattern across all inputs.

LIMITATION 05

Non-expert participants

Participants were not professional 3D product designers, whereas the target users are expert designers working in spatial handover scenarios. Future work should recruit domain experts to test whether the observed trade-offs generalize to professional workflows.
