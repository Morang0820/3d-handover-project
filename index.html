01 — Abstract

Abstract

Three-dimensional product design increasingly relies on asynchronous collaboration across distributed teams and time zones. When a designer begins a new shift, they must review hours of spatial work completed by a remote colleague — understanding what changed, when, and why — before contributing their own work. Existing tools fall short: 2D logs and passive video recordings fail to convey spatial relationships, while full immersive 3D recordings viewed in an HMD cause cybersickness and cognitive overload from rapid scrubbing.

We present a hybrid LED Wall + HMD system that addresses both limitations simultaneously. Designers navigate a 2D keyframe timeline using a mid-air dial gesture and receive visual and haptic cues at keyframes. When a keyframe warrants closer inspection, the designer pulls the corresponding 3D object into the HMD view for immersive examination, then pushes it back to return to 2D navigation. A 3×2 within-subjects pilot study (N = 6) comparing controller, pinch, and dial inputs with and without multimodal cues reveals a compelling dichotomy between objective efficiency and subjective preference, with important implications for XR temporal navigation design.

02 — System

Interaction Design

The interaction follows a four-step flow that keeps immersive use short and purposeful, reducing cybersickness while preserving the spatial richness of 3D design history.

STEP 01

Browse timeline

Scroll through keyframes on the LED Wall using a mid-air dial gesture (wrist rotation).

STEP 02

Receive cues

Visual flash and haptic pulse fire simultaneously at each keyframe, drawing attention without conscious effort.

STEP 03

Inspect in 3D

Pull a moment into 3D space via a pull-to-spawn gesture for closer spatial inspection in the HMD.

STEP 04

Return to 2D

Push-to-dismiss sends the object back; resume 2D timeline scrubbing.

Cognitive Design Rationale

The system separates ventral stream processing (object recognition via foveal keyframe content) from dorsal stream processing (gesture and haptic events), preventing a single channel from being overloaded during simultaneous navigation and content recognition. Keyframe content occupies the foveal zone (0–5°); visual flash cues fire in the parafoveal zone (5–15°) without requiring a saccade; and the full LED Wall timeline provides peripheral context (15°+).

Three cognitive load principles from LaViola et al. are applied: a staged approach (2D overview first, 3D only on demand), a physical metaphor (wrist rotation maps intuitively to timeline scrubbing), and haptic offloading (keyframe arrival delivered through touch, freeing the visual channel for content).

Multimodal Cue Design

Four cue events are defined with uniform intensity across all keyframes. Visual feedback handles continuous state; haptic feedback handles immediate event alerts; audio is reserved for out-of-FoV events only.

03 — Prototype

Prototype

The prototype implements the full interaction pipeline on a physical LED Wall with a Meta Quest 3 HMD (Unity 6000.3.4f1). Three input methods are compared: pinch gesture, controller, and the proposed mid-air dial (wrist rotation). All three control the same 3D timeline bar. Haptic feedback is delivered via bHaptics Tactgloves.

The dial gesture evolved from an initial design using crunched fingers with slight flexion, which caused hand-tracking errors with haptic gloves, to a final design using pinch (index and thumb flexion) for more reliable tracking. The pull-to-spawn / push-to-dismiss interactions are triggered via pinch + z-axis hand movement.

04 — Evaluation

Evaluation Plan

System Evaluation

System performance was evaluated across two latency dimensions prior to the user study.

Hand tracking latency (Meta Quest 3): Average positional error 1.11 cm; latency range 14.4–220.5 ms. Intermittent spikes toward 220 ms risk disrupting perceived continuity of the dial gesture during fine-grained scrubbing.

LED Wall display latency: ~70 ms (measured via debug logging). Cumulative end-to-end latency may be perceptible during rapid navigation and is a non-trivial confound for overshoot rates.

User Study Design

The experiment adopts a 3×2 within-subjects factorial design, crossing three input methods with two cue conditions (6 conditions total). Participants: N = 6 adults aged 20–35 (pilot study); 5 trials per condition, 30 trials total. Statistical analysis used ART ANOVA for parametric metrics and the Friedman test for ordinal data (α = 0.05).

Modality-Dependent Feedback Effect (Question Score)

While main effects were negligible, a large interaction effect between modality and feedback was found for Question Score (F(2, 30) = 2.656, p = .086, η²p = 0.150).

Adding feedback improved scores for Controller (1.83 → 2.33) and Pinch (1.67 → 2.00), but actively decreased scores for Dial (2.33 → 1.50). This suggests a sensory conflict: artificial feedback may clash with the dial's strong inherent physical metaphor.

06 — Discussion

Discussion

Performance vs. Preference

The most striking result is the disconnect between objective efficiency and subjective experience. The controller dominated objectively (large effect sizes for both TKAT and overshoot rate) yet failed to achieve superiority in any subjective metric. Users found the controller the most environmentally disruptive — holding a rigid physical device creates friction when transitioning between timeline interaction and 3D spatial manipulation, breaking the sense of direct engagement with the environment.

Hand-based modalities, though slower, showed clear affordance advantages. Pinch was rated easiest for target finding, likely because direct hand-tracking mimics natural pointing and grasping. However, this naturalness came at the cost of high physical demand, highlighting the well-known "gorilla arm" effect during sustained mid-air gestures. The dial, while slowest, earned the highest willingness-to-use ratings, suggesting that the jog-wheel mental model is intuitive for temporal scrubbing even when mapping gain needs further optimization.

The Modality-Dependent Role of Sensory Feedback

Our findings challenge the assumption that adding sensory feedback universally improves XR interactions. For controller and pinch — where physical constraints or tactile cues are minimal — feedback acted as a "safety net," confirming target acquisition and increasing confidence. For the dial, however, adding feedback actively decreased performance, suggesting that the dial's strong physical metaphor already provides sufficient sensory grounding, and that additional artificial cues cause sensory overload or mismatch rather than assistance.

Design Implications

IMPLICATION 01

Match modality to task demand

For high-speed precision tasks, physical controllers remain unmatched. For ambient or intermittent interactions where immersion matters, well-calibrated pinch is preferable — provided interaction duration is short enough to avoid fatigue.

IMPLICATION 02

Tailor feedback to modality affordances

Sensory feedback should supplement missing tactile information (as with bare-hand gestures) rather than being applied uniformly. Modalities with strong inherent metaphors (dials, physical controls) may be harmed by additional artificial cues.

IMPLICATION 03

Consider adaptive modality switching

Future systems should explore seamlessly transitioning between high-precision controller input and low-friction hand tracking based on the user's immediate context — rapid bulk navigation vs. careful inspection near target keyframes.

Limitations & Future Work

LIMITATION 01

Small pilot study (N = 6)

Limited statistical power means several medium-to-large effects did not reach significance. A larger study with expert designers and richer design histories is needed to confirm observed trends.

LIMITATION 02

Simplified task scenario

The study used a controlled timeline-navigation task rather than a realistic professional handover scenario with annotations, object dependencies, and decision rationales.

LIMITATION 03

Hardware latency confounds

Quest 3 hand tracking introduces intermittent latency spikes (up to 220.5 ms) and the LED Wall added ~70 ms. Future systems should incorporate latency compensation and more stable hand tracking to isolate gesture effects.

LIMITATION 04

Uniform cue design

Results suggest cue utility is modality-dependent, especially for dial. Future work should explore adaptive cue timing and modality-specific feedback rather than applying the same cue pattern across all inputs.
